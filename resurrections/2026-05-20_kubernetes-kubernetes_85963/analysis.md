## Why It Died

The issue failed to gain traction due to complexity and potential backwards compatibility concerns. The Kubernetes RBAC system was designed with allow rules in mind, and adding deny rules required careful consideration of existing workflows and potential security implications.

## Why 2026 Changes Everything

With the advancements in Kubernetes' security features, such as the introduction of ValidatingAdmissionPolicies and improved support for granular access control, the groundwork has been laid for incorporating deny rules into RBAC. Specifically, tools like OPA/Gatekeeper and Kyverno have shown the feasibility of complex policy enforcement.

## Modern Architecture

The modern design could involve introducing a new 'Deny' kind for RBAC rules, which would be evaluated after allow rules. This could utilize existing policy engines like CEL (Common Expression Language) for more flexible rule definitions. Classes/APIs/patterns could include a PolicyEvaluator interface, AllowRule and DenyRule structs, and integration with admission controllers.
